  • Time
    In the beginning, time was everywhere and nowhere.
  • We, humanity, were constrained by the temporality of the present.
  • Actions could only be done sequentially and they took, essentially, as long as they took.
  • Environmental factors dictated our life, from sundown to sunup.
  • Modern means of mechanization had not yet begun to annihilate time. (Solnit 13)
  • We existed as fish swimming through time, with no more need to define and demarcate it than a fish has to define water.
  • Revolution
    Though the assault of mechanism on time had begun decades earlier, with Greek philosophers attacking its philosophical basis (On Truth), the industrial revolution struck a fearsome blow against old Kronos.
  • With the incorporation of modern machinery, we began to annihilate space and time. (Solnit 11)
  • Time was no longer an intractable component of existence.
  • The telegraph could transport ideas and messages instantaneously, with trains reaching nearly that speed—at least relative to previous transport mechanisms. (Standage 59)
  • Production schedules for whole ranges of goods switched from years to hours. (Doppen)
  • Even more importantly, time was no longer an inherent component of a particular entity.
  • While a road might have previously had a distance fixed in time (“3 days by horse”), that time-distance could be consistently shrunken with incremental improvement (the train from Bristol to London got faster over time).
  • We were no longer slaves to Kronos: machines enabled us to fight back against his yoke.
  • Yet, instead of aggressively joining machines in that war, we embraced our former master, firmly enshrining time in our lives.
  • Modern machinery had rapidly compressed the amount of time which it takes for production: the knitting of wool which would have taken days could be done in hours.
  • However, we “shackle[d] ourselves to a schedule” even more fiercely. (Zalewski 7)
  • Clocks became ubiquitous in the workplace, with the Western world converging on a common clock.
  • Productivity and pay was no longer dictated by work accomplished but by time spent.
  • Indeed, the end of the workday is one of the most prolific hours of Marclay’s “The Clock” with hundreds of clips depicting that crucial 5pm hour when the clock (not our bodies or our nature) tells us that work is complete. (Zalewski 8)
  • The modern workplace, as Marclay captures, is driven by the precision of time—despite our relative abundance of it, thanks to labor-saving devices.
  • Nowhere is this clearer than with trains.
  • They represented one of the most aggressive attacks on the tyranny of time: destinations which were previously days away became reachable in a few hours, with modern trains compressing those hours into minutes.
  • Yet, despite this newfound abundance of time, trains treat time as incredibly scarce.
  • As shown by dozens of clips from Marclay’s film, people are constantly rushing to catch trains which left precisely at the hour.
  • Despite the fact that a late train is still hundreds of times faster than walking, we still glance anxiously at our watch when it arrives a few minutes late.
  • Trains are ruled by a harsh and unforgiving timetable which has put time at the center of the train experience: the prototypical conductor is always looking at his pocket-watch.
  • Indeed, railway companies were the first to adopt and demand the standardization of timezones.
  • When the machinery of locomotives assaulted time by compressing distances we chose to elevate time by running our trains strictly by it and constructing ornate alters to it throughout stations.
  • Indeed, Marclay originally hoped to have “The Clock” displayed in the ultimate temple to time: a train station. (Zalewski 15)
  • Enterprise
    Marclay’s production of “The Clock” is thus firmly rooted in the modern relationship with time, which began with the Industrial Revolution.
  • Indeed, its the chronological scale of the piece itself which often most impresses viewers.
  • They ponder how long it must have taken Marclay to construct this piece, with Google not (yet) possessing a digital index of every cinematographic clock. (Smith 6)
  • Fittingly, the production techniques themselves were a testament to time and our modern monument to it.
    • 20
  • In true industrial style, with a budget to match, Marclay hired a number of assistants to parse through dozens of films in search of clocks. (Zalewski 7)
  • He engaged in the fundamental transaction of modern times: paying assistants for their time.
  • In return, on a predetermined & demanding schedule, they dropped off clips of clocks culled from the history of film.
  • Thus, “The Clock” is not merely the work of a singular genius but the result of a modern chronological enterprise wherein labor’s time is converted into art (and, implicitly, money).
  • Film
    Though Marclay’s methods might flirt with time, his medium penetrates to its very core.
  • Film, the moving image, is inherently the application of time to a picture: the sequential viewing of a series of photographs, a simulation of reality. (Solnit 6)
  • Without time, film has no meaning.
  • Indeed, many Hollywood films embrace this fact with plots entirely centered around time, from In Time to Groundhog Day.
  • Film is not merely dependent on time to exist artistically but also socially.
  • Film provides clear demarcations of history, providing a shared sense of time across a society where the individual events of life don’t match.
  • 1977 might have been when you got divorced and I got married, but its also when Star Wars came out for all of us.
  • Thus, film exists as shared measurement of time.
  • Indeed, much of the joy from “The Clock” comes from the occasional recognition of a particular clip (and the associated knowledge of its place in historical time).
  • Through the compilation of film, “The Clock” is a monument to the role which cinematographic time has filled in the modernity of America (Marclay depends almost exclusively on Hollywood clips, as most foreign films fixate on time far less).
  • Deconstruction
    “The Clock” is also a deconstruction of the role of time in film.
  • While American cinema is replete with chronologically-centered films, they all exist on different time scales.
  • Liesl’s midnight is not that of Orson Welles. (Smith 3)
  • Each film constructs its own timescale, a timescale inherent to its functioning as a film—a timespace where it's quicker to make dinner than to fire a gun.
  • To use the term of M.M. Bakhtin, the writers of each film compose their own chronotope.
  • By juxtaposing these films, Marclay destroys the illusion of their different chronotopes.
  • He transforms fictional time into documentary time, turning the unreal ticking of a bomb into the all too real ticking of time. (Brody 2)
  • Yet, in doing so he might also remind us that our “real” time is also constructed: by grafting other times into ours, he makes us wonder whether our time is any more real than those others.
  • This is done most potently, perhaps, with a clip from Easy Rider, where Peter Fonda’s glance at a stopped watch serves to demarcate the real time of 11:40am. (Bradshaw 2)
  • But if a broken fictional watch can accurately tell the real time, then what makes our time any more accurate?
  • Immortality
    Marclay also undermines the capacity of film to be a societal timepiece.
  • While numerous clips provide the tantalizing prospect of rekindling fond memories, acting provides the even more appealing desirable opportunity to transcend time.
  • With their moving image caught in the mechanism of film, actors could possibly exist forever.
  • Man, aided and abetted by machine, could finally defeat time.
  • “The Clock” almost celebrates this victory, but then seizes defeat from its jaws.
  • While we might recognize some clips and certain actors, even more flow by without being recognized.
  • Actors who once thought themselves timeless and immortal have been rendered mortal by the mortality of their audiences.
  • In this, the entropy of the universe is revealed: film can slow down time, but never defeat it.
  • Indeed, Marclay’s exhibition choices play into this by demanding that the viewing of “The Clock” be a shared experience, conducted on couches in a public space.
  • There, the absurdity of immortality is completely demonstrated: while I might recognize certain figures, the presence of other (younger) patrons reveals that the actors mortality rests in my own.
  • By juxtaposing famous and unknown films in front of diverse audiences, Marclay creates a defense of time against the onslaught of mechanized archivalism.
  • Plot
    While certainly aware of the tricks which films play to create plot, Marclay himself deploys those tricks to create a plot for his film: a plot for time itself.
  • By pulling in different clips from the same film, Marclay compresses that film’s plot into his own—in the process, escalating its tension, much like a movie trailer.
  • Indeed, he builds a plot around the rising tensions associated with much of American film.
  • As each hour approaches, a sense of impending climax approaches—yet never comes.
  • Thus, “The Clock” creates a plot of comedy, not tragedy. (Smith)
  • As the film approaches midnight, suspense multiplies with howls and explosions wracking the night.
  • Yet, right after the bell tolls and Big Ben is destroyed, we see the shining timepiece restored.
  • Thus, the comedic message: the plot, and our expectations, are driven solely by the artificially powerful construct of time.
  • As much as we might fear and combat time, it remains intact in the plot of “The Clock.”
  • Sound
    The key to building this plot is sound.
  • While the visual clips are rigidly separated, driven by the harsh dictates of time, Marclay allows sound to bleed to clip to clip.
  • The common rhythm of sound—particularly the tempo of music—pulls together disparate clips.
  • Thus, Marclay does with sound what other directors do with full audiovisual stimulation: he creates a plot through time and sound alone.
  • As the tempo rises near the end of the hour, our expectations rise as well.
  • Yet, this is also a mockery of the existing auditory processes.
  • By crafting a seemingly consistent theme from disparate images, Marclay shows how Hollywood directors use music to manipulate audiences to buy into their time narrative.
  • Indeed, Marclay even changes the meaning of particular clips—from mundane to dramatic, or vice versa—through the sounds which he overlays on top. (Brody 3)
  • For example, a mundane scene from Laura is injected with suspense by silencing everything but the footsteps. (Zalewski 10)
  • Thus, Marclay reveals the common tropes of Hollywood scores.
  • This is particularly evident with the minutes preceding midnight.
  • Marclay doesn’t need to doctor these clips at all: they all naturally include the same screeching violins, a visceral reminder of the limited vocabulary which Hollywood has to metonymically craft a timescale and associated emotions. (Smith 5)
  • Simply through violins and consistent plots, human conceptions of time and the role of midnight have been artificially constructed—a construction which Marclay simultaneously reveals and revels in.
  • Environment
    Even with sound manipulation, Marclay requires a specific environment to sustain the plot.
  • Rather than releasing the video publicly, with virtually anyone able to watch it, Marclay constrains it to public and shared spaces in major cities.
  • This transforms the work from a mere art piece into a cultural artifact.
  • Each installation invites people to come experience it together, sharing in the illusion of its plot and playing off each other's emotions as the essential hours approach.
  • Watching the lead-up to midnight in front of a laptop at home might be dramatic, but the tension wouldn’t have the same palpability as that in a shared public venue.
  • Furthermore, the shared space allows Marclay to have (nearly) complete control over the exact environment of his production, an aspect essential to him. (Zalewski 15)
  • He can ensure that his elaborate soundscape isn’t drowned out by other noises and that the lighting provides a perfect canvas for construction of a visual chronograph.
  • Indeed, Marclay even maintains precise control over the chronological elements.
  • The entire film is encapsulated in a computer program which ensures clips are played in perfect synchronization with local time and that any lapses are corrected immediately. (Zalewski 12)
  • Thus, Marclay’s “The Clock” is a beautiful Swiss watch: beneath the beautiful varnish of art and plot lies technical mastery which truly reveals the point of the production.
  • Through environmental and atmosphere perfectionism, Marclay emphasizes the creation of the perfect chronograph.
  • Story
    The ultimate story of Marclay’s work is that measurement of time is something worth caring about.
  • Perhaps informed by his Swiss roots, Marclay places a premier emphasis on time throughout the work, both in its technical accuracy and its philosophical import.
  • Essential to this is a decision about who owns the time.
  • While Marclay certainly retains a tight grip on the clock itself, the work clearly reveals shared societal ownership of time and our fleeting individual ability to grasp it.
  • Through artistic mastery, he is able to draw in viewers for hours more than they had expected.
  • Yet, perhaps masochistically, we spend those hours by literally watching them fly by.
  • Marclay need not resort to trickery and deception in capturing those hours.
  • The time his film conveys is real, as is the time it takes up, yet we willingly cue up for the privilege of spending it.
  • Indeed, this queuing itself is essential to the narrative of “The Clock.”
  • Rather than allowing for simultaneous viewing online, Marclay requires that the project be rooted firmly in place.
  • Indeed, no two installations ever project the film simultaneously, thus further undermining the concept of synchronicity.
  • While the technology exists today to make simultaneous projection easily, Marclay roots time in space, thus providing a defense for it.
  • After all, if the film could simultaneously show 2am in Tokyo and 12pm in New York, then the shared illusion of objective time is undermined.
  • Speed
  • Marclay's clock begs the question of why we care so much about clocks, why we are obsessed with speed.
  • Only this: time is ticking.
  • Yet, that itself is a powerful message.
  • That time is ticking is a reminder of the mortal condition, of our inability to completely overcome it despite modern devices.
  • Time, ultimately is the presage to death, and that’s a reminder that Marclay willingly conveys.
  • Our machines are losing the battle to overcome time, as are we.
  • We might have built the clocks, but we don’t control them.
  • Like Orson Welles, we’re about to die: we’re “on the minute hand of a clock” which we created.
